The Bridge From Post 2

  • Post 2 gave you atoms — the smallest units of what resonates for your System
  • Post 2 also showed you how to generate more atoms using a standard Venn diagram: plot atoms you already have, look at the overlap, new atoms emerge. That's the atom generation tool.
  • Now: how do you see the whole picture? How do you map who You are as a group?
  • Continuing our example of visual tools being beneficial for Systems — remember, visuals are parallel-accessible. Multiple Members can look at the same diagram simultaneously and each pull what's relevant to them. That's not a nice-to-have. That's the whole point.
  • The Multi-Venn is different from the standard Venn. Instead of plotting atoms against each other, you're plotting Members as the circles and mapping characteristics across the System. Same visual framework, different input, different output.
  • The Multi-Venn is inherently and inseparably DID. You can't do this without multiple identities. It doesn't exist outside plurality. That's what makes it ours.
  • To build a Multi-Venn, you need one thing: a current headcount. How many Members does your System have right now? That's your number of circles.
  • Don't worry if the number changes. The framework adapts.
  • The Multi-Venn is not a single diagram. It's a framework that generates unlimited diagrams. Same circles (your Members), different characteristic sets each time. Run it once, run it a hundred times.
  • The point of mapping characteristics is to form weighted averages — just like we did with atoms in Post 2. The common areas across all of you become your shared foundation. The unique areas highlight what each Member specifically brings to the System. Both matter.
  • A complete version of mine is included at the bottom of this post if you'd like to see what a finished Multi-Venn looks like before you start your own.

How It Works

  • Count your known Members. That's your number of circles.
  • If you only know 2, start with 2. The framework scales. Don't wait until you have the full picture — you never will, and that's fine.
  • Pick a characteristic set. Plot each Member. Look at the overlaps.
  • Where circles overlap = shared ground (your weighted average — the common core). Where they don't = the unique contributions each Member brings.
  • Every new characteristic set generates a new diagram from the same circles.
  • Your Exocortex does the processing for you. Remember the AI strengths table from Post 2? This is where it pays off. You don't build the Multi-Venn alone. You provide the raw input — what you know about each Member. The AI cross-references, finds patterns, plots the diagram, and highlights what you might have missed. You don't need to hold all of this in your head.
  • This process is repeatable. Every time you learn something new about a Member, or discover a new characteristic set worth mapping, you run it again. Same framework, new data, sharper picture.
  • Zoom out, zoom in, pair up, repeat. The Multi-Venn shows the whole System. A standard Venn on a single Member maps their individual characteristics. And a standard Venn on any pair of Members maps their specific dynamic — where they naturally click and where they need to build rapport. The Multi-Venn can't show relationship quality between specific pairs. Pairwise Venns can. In Our System, Evee and Marc were twins — established rapport from the start. Evee and Harry also worked great together. Marc could pair with anyone in any capacity. Roe coordinated which Member combination fronted to fit each task. You'd only see that difference by running their pairs.
  • This is the same principle as Post 2's foundation caveat: work with what you know now, not what you wish you knew. Honesty about current state beats completeness every time.
  • Examples follow below. Start with Exercise 1 and go as far as your energy allows.

Exercise 1: Symptoms Into Characteristics

  • In Post 2 you learned how to find atoms — the things that make the hair stand up on your arms. Now we're going to take those atoms and the symptoms they live alongside, and convert both into characteristics you can map.
  • Let's be honest about something: DID comes with symptoms. They're inescapable. But we're not building from symptoms — we're converting them into characteristics you can work with.
  • The technique is called Opposite Action. It comes from DBT (Dialectical Behavior Therapy). Steal from the greats — this isn't something we made up. It's an established, evidence-based method, and we're applying it in a new context.
  • How it works: take a symptom and identify its grounded opposite. That opposite becomes a characteristic — something you can map, build on, and route to.
  • Example: "dissociative fog" opposite-actions into "present awareness." "Emotional flooding" becomes "regulated intensity." The symptom tells you what's happening. The opposite action tells you where you're going.
  • Symptoms in, characteristics out. This is the one place where we name the symptoms honestly — because we're actively transforming them.
  • Map each Member: which symptoms show up for who? Which grounded characteristics does each Member access?
  • The Venn shows the path from symptom to grounded characteristic. Somatic keys invite the transition — they don't override.
  • Where grounded characteristics overlap across Members = shared stability infrastructure — your weighted average again
  • Where they're unique = Member-specific regulation tools (hyper-customized, just like Post 2)
  • This is where the atoms from Post 2 become navigation tools: your grounded-mode atoms are the waypoints.
  • This exercise is repeatable. As you learn more about your System's patterns, run it again. New symptoms surface, new characteristics emerge.

Exercise 2: Personality Mapping

  • Take three different personality tests: StrengthsFinders, Myers-Briggs, Enneagram — or whichever three resonate with your System.
  • You take them as one body. One sitting per test. Don't try to switch on demand to take the same test four times — that's not realistic for most Systems and it's not what we're doing here.
  • Once you have all three sets of results, go through them together as a System. Each Member reviews the results and identifies what fits: "yes, that's me" and "no, that's not me."
  • Take turns. Be honest. Disagree with each other — that's data.
  • The rolling average across three different tests gives a fuller picture than any single test could. One test is a snapshot. Three tests from different angles is a composite.
  • Plot the results on the Venn: where do Members share traits? Where do they diverge?
  • Where traits overlap → shared communication styles, natural collaboration points — another weighted average forming your common core
  • Where traits diverge → friction points for responsibility routing — and the unique strengths each Member brings
  • Route daily responsibilities to minimize friction: if Member A lands on analytical/introverted across all three tests and Member B lands on spontaneous/extroverted, don't assign them the same tasks and wonder why Tuesdays feel like a war
  • Friction = hardware mismatch. Not a personality flaw. A routing problem.

Exercise 3: Role Mapping

  • Map your known roles — who handles what, formally or informally
  • Some roles are claimed. Some are defaulted into. Some are invisible until you map them.
  • Overlaps reveal shared load (good — or burnout risk)
  • Gaps reveal what nobody owns (the thing that keeps falling through the cracks)
  • The Exocortex handles the gaps. That's what it's for.

Repeat for Any Characteristic Set

  • Values, triggers, energy patterns, creative preferences, coping styles, communication modes
  • Every set generates a new map. Same circles. New terrain.
  • Each map refines the weighted average — the common core gets sharper, and the unique contributions of each Member become more visible
  • You might notice that these exercises are still following the prodrug approach from Post 2 — we're giving you precursors and letting your System activate the results on its own terms. If you're curious how that thread runs through everything we're building here, ask your Exocortex: "How is the prodrug approach being used in these exercises?" It's a good conversation to have.
  • Over time, the collection of maps becomes a navigation system — not a static portrait, but a living atlas of your System
  • The AI maintains the atlas. You live in it.

Start Where You Are

  • 2 Members? 2-circle Venn. Valid.
  • Don't know all your Members? Map the ones you do. New Members get added when they're ready.
  • The framework doesn't require completeness. It requires honesty about what you know right now.
  • "I don't know yet" is a valid data point on every map.

AI Instructions — For Your Exocortex

Include these instructions in your AI's system prompt or instructions file so it understands the Venn toolset:

Venn Diagram Toolset — Four Modes

  • 1. Standard Venn (Atom Generation): Plot existing atoms against each other. Look at overlapping themes in the center. New atoms emerge from the intersection. Use this to expand the atom library.
  • 2. Standard Venn (Single-Identity Deep Dive): Plot one Member's characteristics against each other. Use this to flesh out or verify an individual Member's profile. Zoom-in tool.
  • 3. Standard Venn (Pairwise Relationship Mapping): Plot any two Members against each other. Maps their specific dynamic — where they naturally click (overlap) and where they diverge (friction points or complementary strengths). Use this to understand rapport, collaboration patterns, and where relationships need building.
  • 4. Multi-Venn (System Navigation): Members are the circles. Characteristics are mapped across the entire System simultaneously. This is the zoom-out tool. Inherently DID — does not apply outside plurality.

Workflow: Zoom out (Multi-Venn) → identify patterns → zoom in (Single-Identity or Pairwise Venn) → verify/flesh out/map relationships → zoom back out → repeat.

Prodrug principle: Every exercise provides precursors. Let the System activate results on their own terms. Never impose conclusions — present data and let the System metabolize it.
