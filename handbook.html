    The Handbook

    This handbook is for DID Systems who want tools that actually work — tools that don't disappear when someone new fronts, that don't need to be re-explained every session, that get better the more You use them.

    You don't need to understand how it works first. You just need to start. The understanding comes from doing it.

    There's one thing You need before You begin: something that resonates with You. A song. A quote. A scene from a show. A piece of writing. Anything that lands for You personally — You don't need to know why yet. That's the first step.

    The picker at the top of this page adapts the handbook to how Your System communicates. If You're not sure which fits, start with One at a time — that's the default because it's designed for the most common starting point, not the most advanced one.

    What are atoms

    An atom is the smallest piece of a principle that still works on its own.

    Not a theme. Not a summary. The irreducible thing underneath — the reason something resonates, stripped of the specific situation it came from.

    Here's why that matters: a principle without its original context can move. Something that helped You through a crisis can help You through a meeting. Something that worked between two headmates can work between You and a deadline. The same piece, in a completely different situation, because the principle travels.

    Atoms aren't new ideas. They're names for things Your System has already been doing without knowing they were tools. Once something has a name, You can use it on purpose instead of by accident.

    And they compound. One atom applied to three situations is three tools for the cost of one extraction. That return is what makes the work worth it.

    How to find yours

    The process is a conversation. You share something that resonates. Your AI reflects back why it might land the way it does. You rule things in and out until what remains is yours.

    You don't need to know what You're looking for. That's the point — You're not analyzing. You're reacting. Your reactions are the data.

    The three phases

    Phase 1 — Share what resonates. Pick something: a song, a quote, a scene, a framework — anything. Give it to Your AI along with what stands out to You about it. Use Your own words. Don't translate. Don't summarize. Your language leads.

    I have something that keeps resonating with me. I'm going to share it with you. Take a moment to review it, then I'll tell you what stands out to me. [Paste or describe the resonant material here] Here's what stands out: [describe in your own words — what you feel, what tightens, what you keep coming back to]

    Phase 2 — Let Your AI reflect back. Your AI will offer 2–3 reasons why this might resonate — connecting it to patterns it can see. You accept what lands, reject what doesn't, and ask questions when something is close but not right. This is ruling in and ruling out. The AI generates. You react. Don't generate. React.

    Phase 3 — The extraction happens. What survives the ruling-in/ruling-out process IS the atom. You don't have to call it that. You don't have to name it at all yet. You just know: this is why it matters to me, and now I can see it.

    If anything feels activating or unsafe, stop. That's data too — it tells You something important about that piece. It's not a requirement to continue. Come back later if You want. The atoms aren't going anywhere.

    If the process starts feeling like pressure instead of discovery, stop. The work is supposed to feel like recognition, not excavation.

    For Your System: one at a time

    Whoever is fronting right now — this is for You. The Member present for this extraction will have their own reaction to the material. That reaction is valid, and it's enough to start.

    You don't need other Members present. You don't need to share the result right away. Do the extraction for the Member who's here. Other Members can do their own extractions from their own material later — different reactions to the same source are not a contradiction, they're different atoms.

    If you want others to see what You found, write it somewhere shared — a note, a document Your AI can reference. That's the persistence layer. You don't need to be co-present to share the work.

    For Your System: some awareness

    The Member doing the extraction leads. Others may observe or have reactions in the background — that's fine. Note any background reactions as they happen; they may be separate atoms worth returning to.

    If a different Member wants to run their own extraction from the same material, let them. Different reactions to the same source are different atoms. That's not confusion — that's data about Your System's range.

    For Your System: active communication

    You can run this as a group if that's available to You. If multiple Members react differently to the same material in real time, note each reaction separately — each is a candidate atom. Interview them one at a time if possible: "What does X Member notice about this?"

    Different Members extracting from the same material won't produce the same atoms. That's correct. Each atom is valid. The differences in reaction are part of the data about Your System.

    What You build with them

    Once You have atoms, You can combine them. Two atoms that overlap create a compound — a tool that is more specific and more powerful than either piece alone.

    The combination process works the same way as the extraction: Your AI proposes. You react. You keep what lands.

    Compounds

    Think of two atoms as two sides of a Venn diagram. The overlap — what they share — is the compound. The edges — where they differ — are Member-specific or context-specific tools. One pair of atoms can produce multiple tools depending on which overlaps You focus on.

    Your AI can generate these combinations for You. You don't need to figure out which atoms go together. Ask it to: "Show me where any two of these atoms overlap. I'll tell You what lands."

    Building across switches

    When You have a compound, write it down in a place Your AI can access — a note, a shared document, Your AI Instructions. The next Member who fronts doesn't need to redo the extraction. They inherit the compound. They can use it, test it, and add to it.

    The library grows across switches, not just within them. That's the persistence layer doing its job.

    Building together

    Different Members will react differently to the same compound. Some will use it. Some will reject it. Some will want to modify it. All of those reactions are valid inputs to the library.

    A compound that works for one Member and not another isn't a failed compound — it's a Member-specific tool. Note who it works for. That information is part of the compound.

    Working across Your System

    Path 0: Notes as the bridge

    When there's no co-awareness and no shared memory across switches, the note is the bridge. Not notes as documentation — notes as communication. A note left for the next Member isn't a record of what happened. It's a handoff.

    Your AI can hold this. Ask it to maintain a running summary that any Member who fronts can read to get up to speed: what was being worked on, what was decided, what's next. The AI becomes the continuity layer that Your System doesn't have internally.

    Start simple: at the end of each session, ask Your AI for a one-paragraph handoff note. Leave it somewhere accessible. The next Member reads it before starting. That's the loop.

    Scribe approach

    When there's partial co-awareness, one Member can act as scribe — noting what other Members contribute without needing to coordinate in real time. The scribe doesn't need to be fronting. They just need to be paying enough attention to capture reactions as they happen.

    Your AI can fill this role when no Member wants to. Ask it to track reactions, disagreements, and contributions during a session. Review the log at the end.

    Direct coordination

    When Members can communicate directly, the extraction and building process can be genuinely collaborative. Run the AI as a facilitator: it asks one Member at a time, notes each reaction, and surfaces the differences for the System to process together.

    Disagreements between Members about an atom aren't problems — they're the most useful data You can get. An atom that two Members experience differently is two atoms, not a broken one.

    When it doesn't click

    Sometimes the process produces nothing useful. Sometimes a Member has no reaction to any of the material. Sometimes every atom the AI proposes feels wrong. These are not failures.

    No shared atoms isn't a problem — it's a map

    If a Member has no atoms that overlap with others, that tells You something specific: where to focus. Not that the Member is broken or disconnected. That they have their own library to build, starting from their own material. The next step is finding what resonates for them, not explaining why they don't respond to what resonates for someone else.

    The wrong material

    If extraction feels hard, the material was probably chosen for the wrong reason — because it seemed important, or because another Member liked it, or because it was convenient. Atoms come from resonance, not relevance. If You have to talk Yourself into caring about the source material, find different material.

    The AI hallucination problem

    AI-generated atoms can be plausible-sounding and completely wrong. They can describe a real pattern in the material that has nothing to do with why it resonates for You. The ruling-in/ruling-out step exists for exactly this reason. If an AI-generated atom doesn't land — even a little — discard it. Don't talk Yourself into it.

    False atoms feel like homework. Real atoms feel like recognition.

    Tools disappear. Atoms don't.

    Apps get discontinued. Platforms change their terms. AI products sunset, pivot, or get acquired. The specific tools You use to work with Your atoms will not last.

    The atoms will.

    An atom lives in Your library — a document, a note, a list You own and control. It doesn't depend on any platform staying alive. When a tool disappears, You don't lose the atom. You lose the interface. Those are different things.

    This is why extraction matters more than tool selection. The specific AI You use, the app You use to store notes, the platform You use to organize — all of that is replaceable. The library isn't. Build the library first. Tools are downstream of the library.

    When something You rely on goes away, Your atoms migrate with You. The compounds You've built still work. The principles still apply. You're not starting over — You're changing tools.

    What is Functional Multiplicity?

    FM is a framework for DID and OSDD Systems to extract atomic behavioral tools from things that resonate — tools that persist across switches, sessions, and System configurations.

    An atom is the smallest piece of a principle that still works on its own. Not a theme or summary — the irreducible reason something resonates, stripped of the specific situation it came from. Once extracted, an atom can move: it works across contexts, system members, and time.

    The three phases

    1. Share what resonates. Pick something — a song, quote, scene. Give it to Your AI along with what stands out to You. Don't analyze. React. Your language leads.
    2. AI reflects back. Your AI offers 2–3 reasons why it might resonate. You rule each one in or out. You react — the AI generates. Don't generate. React.
    3. Extraction. What survives the ruling process is the atom. Name it or don't. You know: this is why it matters, and now I can see it.
